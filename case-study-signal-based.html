GovTech SaaS

Signal-Based Outbound
Targeting System

The Problem

GovTech companies serve a narrow buyer — procurement leads, IT directors, and department heads inside government agencies and their contractors. The challenge isn't that the market is too small. It's that most of it is inactive at any given moment. A standard outbound motion that reaches everyone on the list at the same time wastes send volume, burns domain reputation, and produces flat results regardless of message quality.

The goal was to build a system that identifies which accounts are actually worth touching right now, ranks them by fit, and constructs a relevant first touch for each one without requiring a rep to do the research manually for every record.

The Workflow
  1. Pulled the core account universe into Clay — roughly 400 companies that fit the ICP by industry, employee count, and revenue range.
  2. Defined four scoring signals: recent leadership hire in IT or procurement, open headcount in relevant roles, recent contract award or renewal activity, and technology stack indicators suggesting a systems evaluation.
  3. Built Clay enrichment columns for each signal, using a combination of LinkedIn data, job board data, and public contract databases. Each signal returned a binary score (1 or 0) per record.
  4. Stacked the four signals into a composite score. Accounts scoring 3 or 4 entered the active sequence immediately. Scores of 2 went into a watch list. Scores of 0–1 were excluded until signals updated.
  5. Built a modular email library — each signal mapped to a specific opening hook. Clay used the highest-scoring signal per account to select the right module, then Claude via API stitched the hook, a relevance mid-section, and a CTA into a single first touch.
  6. Output: 50 accounts with a fully constructed, signal-matched email ready to send — no manual writing required per record.

The Metrics

What I'd measure if this were running at full scale:

  • Signal-to-reply rate — do accounts scoring 3–4 reply at a meaningfully higher rate than lower-scored accounts?
  • Module performance — which of the four signal hooks generates the most positive responses?
  • List decay rate — how many accounts move from inactive to active week-over-week as signals update?
  • Time-to-send — average time from signal fire to email sent, as a measure of system responsiveness.
